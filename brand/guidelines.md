# Baker Percentage — Brand Guidelines

**Version:** 0.1 (working draft)
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**Status:** Founding document. Everything here is a decision, not a suggestion — but every decision is revisable with reason.

---

## How to read this document

This is opinionated on purpose. A brand guidelines doc that hedges is a brand that hedges. Every section commits to a single direction and explains the trade-off. Where there's a risk, it's named.

The document has five parts:

1. **Strategy** — what we are, who we're for, why anyone cares.
2. **Verbal identity** — how we sound.
3. **Visual identity** — how we look.
4. **Application** — how it lands in the real world.
5. **Operational** — the boring rules that keep it consistent.

# Part 1 — Strategy

## 1.1 The one-line

> **Baker Percentage is a craft tool for home bakers who already know what they're doing.**

That's the whole thing. Every other word in this document serves it.

## 1.2 Offer statement

> **Baker Percentage is the iPhone app for confident home bakers to write, tune, and share their own bread recipes — in the same precise language professional bakeries use.**

This sentence does five jobs:

- **iPhone app** — concrete category.
- **Confident home bakers** — audience filter (not beginners).
- **Write, tune, share** — three verbs in priority order. Recipe authorship is the lead.
- **Their own bread recipes** — ownership and creative agency, not a curated library.
- **Same precise language professional bakeries use** — credibility move. The technical reference (baker's percentages) is the proof.

## 1.3 What the offer gives

A baker who installs Baker Percentage gets:

1. **A recipe authoring tool that speaks in baker's percentages.** Type a hydration, see the math. Tune one ingredient, watch the rest recalculate. Recipes are first-class technical artifacts, not blog posts.
2. **A timing system that survives an 18-hour bulk ferment.** Multi-stage timers, sleep-through-the-night reliability, named stages tied to your recipe — not a generic kitchen timer.
3. **A library of your own recipes that compounds over time.** Versioned, comparable, exportable. Your fifth attempt at country loaf can be diffed against your first.
4. **A peer-level community of bakers who share recipes the same way you write them.** Not a forum. A library of structured, comparable recipes from other bakers, with discussion attached to specific stages and ingredients.

## 1.4 Benefits

Two tiers, both important. Don't let one crowd out the other.

### Rational benefits (what we promise the head)

- **Precision** — the math is correct, every time. Hydration, salt percentage, levain ratios.
- **Reliability** — timers will not fail. Recipes will not be lost. Your library is yours.
- **Comparability** — you can hold two recipes side-by-side and see the difference in numbers, not vibes.
- **No noise** — no ads, no SEO bait, no recipe preambles about your grandmother. The recipe is the point.

### Emotional benefits (what we promise the gut)

- **Confidence** — the app treats you as a peer. You don't get talked down to.
- **Pride of authorship** — the recipes you write feel like work. Like something a real bakery would publish.
- **Calm** — long ferments are stressful. The app holds the timing so you can stop checking your phone every twenty minutes.
- **Belonging** — there's a small, real community of people who think about hydration the way you do.

## 1.5 Audience

### Primary user — and the only one we design for

**The Confident Hobbyist.** Bakes regularly (weekly or more). Already understands baker's percentages, autolyse, bulk fermentation, cold retard. Owns a Dutch oven, a banneton, probably a scale that reads to 1g. Frustrated by recipe blogs and the chaos of online communities. Wants to *write their own recipes* and have them taken seriously.

Demographics are secondary; psychographics are everything. This person:

- Reads cookbooks cover-to-cover.
- Has opinions on Tartine vs. Forkish.
- Has tried at least three different starter feeding schedules.
- Will pay for tools they respect.

### Who Baker Percentage is *not* for

- **First-timers.** The app does not teach you what hydration is. It assumes you know. (If we ever build a teaching layer, it lives in a separate product or a clearly-marked beginner mode — not in the brand.)
- **Casual recipe-hunters.** People who want "an easy bread recipe" are better served by Yummly.
- **Cake and pastry-only bakers.** We are a bread app. Sourdough is the center. Yeasted and enriched doughs are welcome; cakes are not.

This is uncomfortable because it excludes paying customers. **Do not soften it.** The narrowness is the moat.

## 1.6 Positioning statement

> **For** confident home bakers who already work in baker's percentages,
> **Baker Percentage** is the iPhone app
> **that** lets them write, tune, and share bread recipes in the precise language of professional baking,
> **unlike** recipe apps and baking blogs,
> **because** it treats every recipe as a versioned, comparable technical document — not content.

## 1.7 Personality

If Baker Percentage were a person:

- Reads *Modernist Bread* for pleasure.
- Has strong opinions, expresses them in two sentences, doesn't repeat them.
- Knows the numbers but doesn't lecture.
- Wears a clean apron.
- Asked you to taste the crumb without saying anything first.
- Has nothing to prove and nothing to sell.

**Adjectives (use these as litmus):**
Precise. Quiet. Confident. Craft-respecting. Dry-witted. Generous (to peers). Unimpressed (by bullshit).

**Anti-adjectives (if a draft feels like any of these, rewrite):**
Cute. Bouncy. Bubbly. Wholesome. Aspirational. Beginner-friendly. Cozy. Hand-holdy.

## 1.8 Brand pillars

Three. Every campaign, screen, feature, and post should advance at least one. If it doesn't, kill it.

1. **The math is the story.** We are the only app that puts baker's percentages on the front of the recipe, not in a footnote. Numbers are beautiful. Percentages are the format.
2. **Your recipes, taken seriously.** The recipes you write deserve to look like a bakery's spec sheet, not a blog post. We design recipes the way a watchmaker designs a movement diagram.
3. **Time, held for you.** Bread is timing. We are the app that won't drop your bulk ferment because the screen locked. The boring infrastructure of reliability is, secretly, the emotional core.

## 1.9 Values

These are not on a poster. They are tie-breakers when decisions are close.

- **Signal over volume.** A small library of beautiful recipes beats a large library of average ones. Discoverability follows quality, not the other way.
- **The unit is the recipe.** Not the user, not the post, not the like. The recipe is what we are organized around.
- **Defaults matter more than features.** What we ship turned on, set to, named, and ordered — that is the brand. Toggles are an admission of defeat.
- **No ads, ever.** This is not a value, it's a contract.

# Part 2 — Verbal identity

## 2.1 Voice

Three modifiers, in order of priority:

1. **Precise.** We say what we mean with no padding. Numbers where numbers help. No "delicious," no "amazing," no "perfect loaf."
2. **Dry.** Wit comes from understatement, not exclamation. We're funny because we don't try to be.
3. **Peer-level.** We talk to the user the way one experienced baker talks to another. No instructions disguised as enthusiasm.

## 2.2 Tone

Tone shifts by surface. Voice does not.

| Surface                     | Tone                          | Example                                                             |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Marketing / landing page    | Confident, declarative        | "Recipes in percentages. The way bakeries write them."              |
| In-app empty states         | Generous, brief               | "You haven't saved a recipe yet. Start with a hydration."           |
| Timer notifications         | Calm, factual, action-first   | "Bulk ferment ends in 5 min. Shape next."                           |
| Errors                      | Direct, no apology theater    | "Couldn't save. Tap to retry."                                      |
| Onboarding (the only place we soften) | Welcoming, still precise | "Set your baseline flour weight. We'll do the percentages from here." |
| Community guidelines        | Firm, generous, no jargon-hazing | "Recipes get critiqued, not bakers. Numbers help."                |

## 2.3 Vocabulary

### Yes

`hydration` · `levain` · `bulk` · `shape` · `score` · `crumb` · `oven spring` · `pre-shape` · `final shape` · `retard` · `proof` · `autolyse` · `bake` · `formula` · `ratio` · `percentage` · `gram`

### No

`yummy` · `delicious` · `perfect` · `easy peasy` · `foolproof` · `step-by-step` · `tutorial` · `guide` · `journey` · `level up` · `unlock` · any food emoji in body copy · "Bread is *life*" or any variant

### Names for things in the product

- A user-created recipe is a **formula**. (Pro bakeries call them this. We follow.)
- A version of a formula is a **revision**, numbered. (Not "v2," not "copy.")
- A timer attached to a stage is a **stage timer** or just **stage**. (Not "alarm" in-product — alarms are how the OS phrases the notification, not how we phrase the feature.)
- A shared formula visible to others is **published**.
- A group of formulas a baker is iterating on is a **batch**.

This vocabulary is brand-defining. Use it everywhere — in the app, in marketing, in the community.

## 2.4 Tagline candidates

Three. Each does a different job. Pick one for launch.

1. **"Recipes in percentages."** — *Recommended.* Three words. States the product, the differentiator, and the audience filter simultaneously. Functions as a logo lockup.
2. **"The math is the recipe."** — More poetic, more about the philosophy. Better for hero shots than for the App Store subtitle.
3. **"Bread, by the numbers."** — Plays well on social. Slightly more accessible. Slightly less specific.

**Do not** use: "Bake better bread," "Your bread, perfected," "From flour to loaf," or anything ending in "made simple."

## 2.5 Manifesto

This is the founding text. It goes on the About page, in the first email, on a wall behind the founders if there ever is one. Long-form voice reference.

> **Bread is math.**
>
> Every bakery in the world writes its recipes the same way: flour is 100%, and every other ingredient is a percentage of that flour. It's the most useful piece of notation in any kitchen. It lets you scale a recipe up, down, or sideways. It lets you compare two recipes without converting cups to grams. It lets you talk to another baker, anywhere, and be understood.
>
> Most baking apps don't use it.
>
> Most baking apps treat bread like a blog post: a story, a hero image, a list of cups and tablespoons, and a comment section that calls everything "amazing." We built Baker Percentage because we wanted the opposite. A place to write your own recipes, in the language bakeries use, with the math done for you. A library that gets sharper every time you bake. A few quiet peers who'll tell you the truth about your crumb.
>
> Baker Percentage is for the home baker who's stopped Googling recipes and started writing their own. You already know what hydration means. You already weigh your salt. You already know that the second proof matters more than the first one.
>
> We made an app for you.

# Part 3 — Visual identity

## 3.1 Logo system

### Wordmark

The primary logo is the word **"Baker Percentage"** set in **Fraunces** (Regular weight, optical size set for display), with a custom-treated **`%`** glyph as the focal character — or as a standalone symbol.

The `%` is the symbol. The whole identity orbits it.

### Symbol

The **`%`** mark — the existing app icon — is the brand symbol. It is the single most ownable asset in the system and appears everywhere the brand does: app icon, favicon, social profile, lockups, swag, future bakery collabs.

**System principles (the mark itself is decided by the designer; these are the rules for how it lives in the system):**

- The symbol must survive from **16×16 px** (favicon, in-app nav) up to building-scale signage without redrawing — one master vector, optically corrected at small sizes if needed.
- The mark is never decorated, never set inside a circle/rounded-rect background outside the app icon container, never combined with a wheat stalk, loaf illustration, or chef's hat.
- The mark is rendered in Char on Crumb by default. Inverted (Crumb on Char or Crumb on Rye) is allowed; **no other color combinations exist.**
- The mark is not Ember. Ember is reserved for action and state.

### Lockups

1. **Primary (vertical):** `%` symbol stacked above the wordmark. App launch screen, App Store icon source.
2. **Horizontal:** `%` symbol left of the wordmark, separated by a 1× counter-width gap. Marketing headers.
3. **Symbol-only:** for app icon, favicon, social profile images, swag.
4. **Wordmark-only:** for footers, fine print, and contexts where the symbol is repeated elsewhere on the surface.

### Clear space

Minimum clear space around any lockup: the height of the lowercase "e" in the wordmark. Never less.

### Minimum sizes

- Symbol-only: **16px** (favicon), **24px** (in-app nav)
- Wordmark: **80px** wide
- Horizontal lockup: **120px** wide

### Don'ts

- Don't stretch, skew, or rotate the symbol.
- Don't set the wordmark in any other typeface.
- Don't place the wordmark on a busy photograph without a solid backplate.
- Don't add a "tagline pill" beneath the lockup. The tagline lives separately.
- Don't ever combine the `%` with a wheat stalk drawing, a bread loaf illustration, or a chef's hat. Ever.

## 3.2 Color

### The palette

Six named colors. Each has one job. Each color holds its hex regardless of context — the two mid-grays (**Flour** and **Starch**) are distinct atomic colors with distinct roles, not modal variants of a single token.

| Name       | Hex        | Role                                                                                  |
| ---------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Crumb**  | `#F4EDE0`  | Primary background (light, default). Primary text on dark.                            |
| **Char**   | `#1A1614`  | Primary text on Crumb. Headings, body, recipe content.                                |
| **Rye**    | `#2A1F1A`  | Primary background (dark mode only). Warm cocoa-paper, not OLED black.                |
| **Flour**  | `#6E665C`  | Secondary text and hairlines **on Crumb** (light mode). Deep warm taupe.              |
| **Starch** | `#A8A096`  | Secondary text and hairlines **on Rye** (dark mode). Pale, fine, refined warm gray.   |
| **Ember**  | `#C8421C`  | Single accent. Used surgically — see usage rules.                                     |

### Critical decision: **light mode is the default.**

Most food apps default to cream-and-sage wellness palettes. Most tech apps default to OLED-black + neon accent. Both look generic in 2026. We sit in the third place — **editorial print**: warm paper, deep ink, hot accent, mono numbers. The brand reads like a cookbook page or a bakery's printed spec sheet, not a wellness brand and not an AI startup.

Dark mode is supported as a respected system setting. It uses Rye as the canvas (warm and paper-like, not cold) so the dark version still feels like the same brand printed on darker stock — not a different product.

### Surface rules (light, default)

- **Background:** Crumb across the entire viewport. No off-color hero sections, no full-bleed Ember blocks.
- **Cards and elevated surfaces:** **same Crumb, separated by a 1px Flour hairline.** No drop shadows, no tinted card fills. The editorial discipline is in the hairlines.
- **Dividers:** 1px Flour at 30% opacity.
- **Pure white (`#FFFFFF`) is forbidden.** It reads as clinical. Crumb is the lightest surface in the system.

### Ember usage rules

Ember is the single accent. It is loud on purpose. Therefore:

- Use Ember for the **one** most important action per screen — never two.
- Use Ember for timer-active states (a stage in progress).
- Use Ember for the live-recalculating number on the recipe screen (the one currently being edited).
- Never use Ember for body copy.
- Never use Ember on backgrounds larger than ~10% of the viewport.
- Never combine Ember with any other accent color. There is no other accent color.

### Semantic system (built from the palette — no new colors)

- **Success:** Char on Crumb with a 1px Char hairline. The completion is signaled by structure, not color.
- **Warning:** Ember at 70% opacity, with the word "Warning" or equivalent state label.
- **Error:** Ember at full strength with a 1px Ember border around the affected field.
- **Disabled:** Flour at 40% opacity (light mode); Starch at 40% opacity (dark mode).

If a designer asks for a green for success, the answer is no. Success in this brand is calm, not celebratory.

## 3.3 Typography

Three typefaces. All open-source. All free. All on Google Fonts (or self-hosted from the open-source releases).

### Display: **Fraunces**

A variable serif with character. Used for: wordmark, recipe titles, hero copy, marquee moments. Weights: Regular (400) and SemiBold (600). Optical size adjusted per surface.

**Why Fraunces:** soft serifs that feel handcrafted but precision-cut. Variable axes let us pull personality up or down. Free and open-source.

### Body / UI: **Geist Sans**

A precise neo-grotesque sans. Used for: all UI labels, body copy, navigation, buttons. Weights: Regular (400), Medium (500), SemiBold (600).

**Why Geist Sans:** clean, machine-like rhythm without coldness. Pairs cleanly with Fraunces. Free.

### Numbers: **Geist Mono** — the signature move

Used **exclusively** for: baker's percentages, gram weights, hydration values, temperatures, times, durations, oven settings, recipe ratios.

This is the most ownable typographic move in the brand. Every number in the app, in marketing, on the landing page is set in mono. It makes recipes look like spec sheets. It makes the math feel like math.

**Rule:** if it's a quantity, it's in Geist Mono. If it's prose, it's in Geist Sans. There is no third option.

### Type scale (mobile-first, iOS)

| Token        | Typeface       | Size  | Weight | Line height | Use                          |
| ------------ | -------------- | ----- | ------ | ----------- | ---------------------------- |
| `display-l`  | Fraunces       | 48    | 400    | 1.05        | Hero, splash                 |
| `display-m`  | Fraunces       | 32    | 400    | 1.1         | Recipe title                 |
| `display-s`  | Fraunces       | 24    | 600    | 1.15        | Section header               |
| `body-l`     | Geist Sans     | 17    | 400    | 1.45        | Long-form prose              |
| `body`       | Geist Sans     | 15    | 400    | 1.45        | Default body                 |
| `body-s`     | Geist Sans     | 13    | 400    | 1.4         | Captions, metadata           |
| `label`      | Geist Sans     | 12    | 500    | 1.2         | UI labels, all caps OK       |
| `mono-l`     | Geist Mono     | 24    | 500    | 1.1         | Big numbers (hero hydration) |
| `mono`       | Geist Mono     | 15    | 400    | 1.3         | Inline percentages           |
| `mono-s`     | Geist Mono     | 12    | 400    | 1.3         | Recipe table cells           |

## 3.4 Iconography

**Custom icon set, monoline, 1.5px stroke at 24px.** Geometric, not illustrative. No filled icons except for active-state replacements of the same outline icon.

Reference vibe: Phosphor Icons (Regular weight). Not SF Symbols filled style — too soft.

**No food-themed icons in the system.** No wheat stalks, loaves, rolling pins, chef's hats. The brand says "bread" through structure, not picture-language.

## 3.5 Photography

Used sparingly. Two contexts only:

1. **Recipe cover (optional, baker-supplied).** Top-down crumb shots, single light source, warm shadow. No styled-board flat-lays. Backgrounds: natural wood, parchment, or solid Crumb/Char.
2. **Marketing hero (rare).** One image per campaign. Tight crops on a single loaf — score, ear, crumb interior. The bread is the subject. No hands holding bread. No "lifestyle." No people in aprons smiling.

**Forbidden:**

- Stock photography.
- Hands tearing bread.
- Wheat fields.
- Wine glasses next to loaves.
- "Mediterranean" lifestyle imagery.

## 3.6 Illustration

**No illustration system.** The brand says it with type, color, and the `%` mark. Adding an illustration style would undercut the editorial discipline of the rest of the system.

The single exception: technical diagrams (folding patterns, scoring diagrams, dough-shaping steps) when explicitly useful in a recipe. These are **functional drawings**, not decoration: monoline, in Char or Crumb only, never Ember, never with personality.

## 3.7 Motion

Three principles. Motion is restrained on purpose.

1. **Numbers animate; layouts don't.** When a baker's percentage updates because the user changed flour weight, the number counts up. The recipe doesn't slide or fade. The math is what's alive.
2. **Timers are precise, not playful.** Countdowns tick in monospace. The progress ring is a single Ember stroke that completes — no spring, no overshoot, no celebratory burst at 0.
3. **One transition language: ease-out cubic, 200ms.** Used everywhere. No long page transitions. No parallax. No "wow" animations. The app respects the baker's attention.

The landing page (a separate web property) has more freedom — but the same restraint. Lenis-driven smooth scroll, GSAP-driven micro-animations on percentages and weights *being typed in by a typing-cursor*, no full-screen video, no scroll-jacked story.

# Part 4 — Application

## 4.1 App icon

The existing `%` mark, as designed by the founder. The icon is a flag — recognizable at a glance on a home screen. No gradient, no decoration, no app-store tagline overlay.

Standard exports: iOS Light (the canonical version) and iOS Tinted (system-tinted variants for iOS 18+). A Dark variant is provided for users who choose dark home screens; it mirrors the Light icon with inverted figure-ground (Crumb mark on Rye or Char). No alternate icons at launch.

## 4.2 Key in-app moments

### Recipe screen (the most important surface in the entire product)

- Title in Fraunces, 32pt.
- A row of three large mono numbers immediately under the title: **hydration**, **total flour weight**, **salt %**. These are the recipe's signature.
- Ingredient list as a two-column table: ingredient (Geist Sans) and percentage (Geist Mono).
- A right-aligned **gram** column toggle (the percentages are primary; grams are the conversion).
- Stages as a vertical list, each with a duration in mono and a "start timer" affordance.

**The recipe screen is the brand.** If a screenshot of this screen doesn't immediately look unlike every other recipe app, the design has failed.

### Timer-running state

- Background stays Crumb. The canvas does not shift — the brand does not "go dark" to signal urgency. (Going dark for active states is a tech-app cliché we explicitly avoid.)
- A 2px Ember progress ring around the stage time remaining is the *only* color change on the screen.
- Time remaining in `mono-l`, Char.
- The active stage row gets a 1px Ember left-rule (4px wide, full row height) — the rest of the stage list stays Char-on-Crumb with Flour hairlines.
- One action available: "End early" — Flour, bottom of screen, no Ember.

### Community feed

- Vertical list of **formulas**, not posts.
- Each row: formula title (Fraunces), baker's name (Geist Sans), and the three signature numbers in mono.
- No images in the feed by default. Images are a tap away.

This last point is provocative. **Most social feeds in food are image-led.** Ours is number-led — because the numbers are what differentiate one bread from another to this audience. Test it; if it fails, fall back to a single thumbnail per row, but never the full grid.

## 4.3 Landing page hero (web)

The hero is a working calculator.

- Big number in mono, animated: **"75%"**.
- Subhead in Fraunces: **"Hydration."**
- Below: a fake ingredient table that updates as the hydration number is dragged or auto-animates.
- Tagline below the calculator: **"Recipes in percentages."**
- Single CTA: "Get it on the App Store."

No video. No carousel of testimonials. No "as seen in." The product *is* the hero.

## 4.4 Social / launch posts

Three formats. That's it.

1. **The Number Card.** A single huge mono number in Char on Crumb, the formula name in Fraunces below. A 1px Flour hairline frames the card edge. Tap-through to a published formula. (Instagram, Threads.)
2. **The Recipe Spec.** A screenshot of a full recipe screen, rendered as an image. The screen is so distinctive it functions as marketing without context. (Instagram, Pinterest.)
3. **The Sentence.** Just text. Geist Sans, Char on Crumb, one short line. ("Hydration is the only ingredient that matters." — pinned reply: "Disagree. Salt.") (Twitter/X, Threads.)

An inverted variant of each format (Crumb-on-Rye) exists for occasional contrast — used sparingly, no more than one in five posts, to keep the brand canvas reading as paper.

No reels with trending audio. No memes. No carousel listicles.

## 4.5 Don'ts gallery (anti-references)

If a design ever looks like any of these, stop:

- A baking blog with a hero image, a personal anecdote, and a "jump to recipe" button.
- Yummly's recipe cards (square thumbnails, three-line truncated titles, ratings).
- A Duolingo-style mascot waving at the user.
- A cream-and-sage "modern wellness" palette with script accents and pastel terracotta. (Our Crumb is warm paper, not pastel; our Char is true deep ink, not soft brown; our Ember is hot, not muted.)
- A Linear/Vercel-style OLED-black UI with a neon accent. (Tech-app dark mode is what we are explicitly *not* doing.)
- A serif-and-script logo with a wheat-stalk underline.
- Any onboarding flow longer than three screens.

# Part 5 — Operational

## 5.1 Recipe formatting standard

Every published recipe in Baker Percentage follows this schema. This is brand-defining; treat changes as you would changes to the logo.

```
[Formula name]                        — Fraunces, display-m
By [baker], rev. [n]                  — Geist Sans, body-s, Flour

HYDRATION    TOTAL FLOUR    SALT
75%          1000g          2.0%      — Geist Mono, mono-l, three columns

INGREDIENTS
Bread flour            85%   850g
Whole wheat            15%   150g
Water                  75%   750g
Levain                 20%   200g
Salt                   2.0%  20g

STAGES
Autolyse               60min
Bulk ferment           4h    @ 26°C
Pre-shape              20min
Final shape            —
Cold retard            12h   @ 4°C
Bake                   45min @ 250°C → 230°C
```

## 5.2 Community ritual / contribution language

The community is structured, not free-form. The contribution UI enforces the recipe schema above. You can't post a paragraph; you publish a formula.

Discussion attaches to the formula at the **stage** level, not the post level. A comment lives on "Bulk ferment" or "Final shape," not on "Sandra's country loaf." This makes critique specific and reduces noise.

Community language always:

- Refers to the user as **baker** (in second person: "you"; in third: "the baker").
- Refers to their content as **formulas**, never posts or recipes.
- Uses "publish" and "revise," not "post" and "edit."

## 5.3 Naming for features

Names should be either **a baker's word** or **a precise English word**. Never marketing-speak.

| What it does                                | We call it     | We do NOT call it                |
| ------------------------------------------- | -------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Multi-stage timer system                    | Stages          | Smart Timer, Bread Timer Pro     |
| User's recipe library                       | Formulas        | My Recipes, My Cookbook          |
| Saving a copy as a new version              | Revise         | Duplicate, Save As               |
| Making a formula visible to others          | Publish        | Share, Post                      |
| A collection of related formulas in-progress | Batch           | Folder, Collection               |
| Following another baker                     | Follow         | (this one we don't reinvent)     |
| The community surface                       | The Feed       | Community, Discover, Explore     |

## 5.4 Accessibility

This is not a section to skim.

- **Contrast — light mode (default).**
  - **Char on Crumb:** ~16.0 — AAA at all sizes. The default body pair.
  - **Flour (`#6E665C`) on Crumb:** ~5.4 — AA for normal text. Use for secondary text and captions.
  - **Ember on Crumb:** ~5.5 — AA for normal text; AAA for large text. Reserved for accent and action, not body.
- **Contrast — dark mode.**
  - **Crumb on Rye:** ~13.9 — AAA at all sizes.
  - **Starch (`#A8A096`) on Rye:** ~5.9 — AA for normal text.
  - **Ember on Rye:** ~6.0 — AA for normal text; AAA for large text.
- **Font sizing.** All type respects the user's iOS Dynamic Type setting. The scale in §3.3 is the *default*; never hard-code px.
- **Mono numbers.** Geist Mono is tabular by default — verify all weight digits align. Critical for the recipe screen.
- **Timers.** Every active timer must announce its state to VoiceOver every 60 seconds and immediately on completion. A baker shouldn't have to look at the screen during a bulk ferment to know where they are.
- **Color is never the only signal.** Active timers are signaled by Ember *and* by a state label ("Running") *and* by a left-rule on the row. Errors are Ember *and* the word "Error."

# Appendix A — Risks to navigate

A real brand has real failure modes. Three to watch.

### Risk 1: The name forecloses growth.

"Baker Percentage" filters out beginners. That's intentional. But if the business needs to broaden — to teaching, to other doughs, to a cooking app — the name will fight. **Mitigation:** don't broaden under this name. Sub-brands or sister products if growth demands it.

### Risk 2: Light-mode-default in food sits next to every wellness brand.

Cream-and-sage with terracotta accents is the dominant food/wellness palette. A casual glance at our screenshots could lump us in. **Mitigation:** the editorial discipline does the differentiation, not the canvas. Mono numbers on every percentage. 1px Flour hairlines instead of card fills. Hot Ember (not muted terracotta). Deep Char ink (not soft brown). Fraunces with crisp optical-display weights (not script). If a designer ever proposes softening any of these "to fit the category," that's the moment to refuse.

### Risk 3: The peer-level voice can read as cold.

We've forbidden words like "delicious" and "amazing." A casual reader skimming marketing might find the tone arch. **Mitigation:** the manifesto and onboarding compensate. Both surfaces are explicitly warmer (still not warm) so a first-time visitor gets one moment of welcome before the precision takes over.

# Appendix B — What this brand will NOT do

Decided in advance, so we don't relitigate.

- We will not run ads.
- We will not accept paid recipe placements.
- We will not add a beginner mode that ships under the Baker Percentage name.
- We will not gamify (no streaks, no badges, no XP).
- We will not use AI-generated photography of bread.
- We will not add a generic "kitchen timer" feature unattached to a stage.
- We will not localize the recipe schema. Baker's percentages are universal.
- We will not collaborate with a celebrity baker. Bakers, yes. Celebrities, no.
