# Baker Percentage — Brand Guidelines

**Version:** 1.2
**Date:** 2026-05-28
**Status:** Working brand document. Every decision here is a commitment, not a suggestion — but every commitment is revisable with reason.

## How to read this document

Four parts: strategy, verbal identity, visual identity, operational essentials. Plus two appendices: risks to navigate, and the things this brand has already committed to never doing.

Implementation specifics — hex codes, typeface choices, sizing tokens, screen layouts — live in the design system, not here. This document commits to *what* the brand is and *why*. The *how* is downstream.

Every section commits to a single direction. Where there's a risk, it's named.

# Part 1 — Strategy

## 1.1 The one-line

> **Baker Percentage is a craft tool *and a diary* for home bakers who already know that the recipe isn't the bread.**

Everything in this document serves that sentence. The italicized *and a diary* is the heart of the brand — the recipe is one half of the practice; the bake itself is the other half, and the bake is what the diary holds.

## 1.2 The two registers

The brand has two voices and two visual languages, both real, both Baker Percentage, deployed at different moments.

| Register      | When it speaks                                                       | What it sounds and looks like                                                                |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Formula**   | The recipe. The math. The artifact you author and publish.           | Editorial. Precise. Mono numerals. Hairlines. Lighter paper.                                  |
| **Bake**      | The diary. The process. The notes you take during and after a bake.  | Warm. Handed. Slower. Older paper. Italic serif for personal moments. Photos dropped in like polaroids. |

These don't fight. A baker's notebook has both: a typed formula on one page, scribbled notes on the next. The brand earns its dimensionality by holding both without compromising either.

**Rule:** every brand surface answers the question *which register?* before any design decision is made. A recipe screen is Formula. A diary entry is Bake. The home-screen feed is Bake. A published formula is Formula. Marketing copy is usually Bake — the warm register sells better than the cold one, except when we're showing the recipe artifact itself.

## 1.3 Offer statement

> **Baker Percentage is the iPhone app for home bakers to write recipes in baker's percentages, set timers that survive an overnight ferment, and keep a diary of every bake — so that the bread you make next month is specifically, measurably, better than the bread you made last month.**

Five things this does:

- **iPhone app** — concrete category.
- **Home bakers** — not professionals, not first-timers. The audience filter.
- **Write recipes / set timers / keep a diary** — three verbs, three product surfaces, three brand pillars in disguise.
- **Better than last month** — the time dimension. The app gets more valuable the longer you use it. The promise is the practice you keep, not better bread today.
- **Specifically, measurably** — the precision is real. The math is the floor.

## 1.4 What the offer gives

A baker who installs Baker Percentage gets:

1. **A recipe authoring tool in baker's percentages.** Type a hydration, see the math. Tune one ingredient, watch the rest recalculate. Recipes are first-class technical documents — *formulas* — versioned, comparable, exportable.
2. **A timing system that survives an 18-hour bulk ferment.** Multi-stage timers tied to your recipe's named stages. Sleep-through-the-night reliability. Wakes you for the next step, not earlier.
3. **A diary that records every bake.** Each bake is its own entry, attached to a formula. Photos, notes, ambient temperature, what felt tight, what you'd do differently. The diary is private by default; you can publish a bake alongside the formula if you want others to see.
4. **A library that compounds over time.** Your fifth attempt at country loaf can be diffed against your first — both the formula changes and the bake notes. Across a year of bakes, your bread becomes specifically yours.
5. **A peer community of bakers who keep the same kind of notebook.** Curated, structured, low-volume. People publish formulas; sometimes they publish bake notes too. Discussion attaches to specific ingredients or stages. No engagement bait.

## 1.5 Benefits

Two tiers. Both critical. Don't let one crowd out the other.

### Rational benefits

- **Precision where precision helps** — the math is correct. Hydration, salt, levain ratios, total dough weight.
- **Reliability where it matters** — timers will not fail. Recipes will not be lost. Bakes are not deleted by an algorithm.
- **Comparability over time** — your bakes are diffable. You can see what changed and what stayed.
- **No noise** — no ads, no SEO bait, no streaks, no badges, no "you haven't baked in 3 days" guilt notifications.

### Emotional benefits

- **Belonging in your kitchen** — your bread doesn't need to imitate a bakery to be good. Your kitchen is the place. Your conditions are the conditions. The brand respects this.
- **A practice you keep** — bread takes time, and so does becoming a baker. The diary makes the time visible.
- **Hand-respect** — the app doesn't pretend to know more than you do about the dough in front of you right now. It does the math; you do the bake.
- **Quiet pride** — every loaf you make is documented in a notebook only you (and the bakers you choose to share with) can read. There's dignity in that.

## 1.6 Audience

### Primary user

**The Confident Hobbyist.** Bakes regularly. Already understands baker's percentages, autolyse, bulk fermentation, cold retard. Owns a Dutch oven, a banneton, a scale that reads to 1g. Frustrated by recipe blogs and the chaos of online communities. Wants their recipes to be taken seriously.

The audience also knows the recipe isn't the bread. They've baked a hydration that worked in winter and failed in July. They've followed a formula to the gram and gotten a different loaf than the photo. They are not first-timers chasing certainty — they are practitioners who want the math AND a place to keep notes on the gap between the math and the loaf.

### Who Baker Percentage is *not* for

- **First-timers** chasing certainty. The app doesn't teach you what hydration is, and it doesn't promise that following the recipe guarantees the bread.
- **Casual recipe-hunters.** Yummly is better for that.
- **Cake-and-pastry bakers.** Bread is the center. Cake recipes are deterministic and Baker Percentage will feel slow and over-built for them.
- **Anyone who wants the app to coach them through a bake.** We are a witness and a journal, not a coach. If you want notifications telling you when to fold, you want a different app.

Narrow on purpose. The narrowness is the moat.

## 1.7 Positioning statement

> **For** confident home bakers who already work in baker's percentages and know the recipe isn't the bread,
> **Baker Percentage** is the iPhone app
> **that** holds their formulas, their timers, and a diary of every bake,
> **unlike** recipe apps and baking blogs,
> **because** it treats bread as the math AND the hand — and your kitchen as the place that matters.

## 1.8 Personality

If Baker Percentage were a person:

- Reads *Modernist Bread* for pleasure but doesn't quote it at parties.
- Owns one expensive proofing basket and three cheap ones.
- Knows the numbers. Doesn't lecture about them.
- Has a notebook with flour on the page corner.
- Asked you to taste the crumb without saying anything first.
- Was a little disappointed in their last bake. Has theories.

**Adjectives (use these as litmus):**
Precise where it matters · Patient · Hand-aware · Confident without being cold · Craft-respecting · Dry-witted · Generous to peers · Unimpressed by bullshit · Kitchen-grounded.

**Anti-adjectives (if a draft feels like any of these, rewrite):**
Cute · Bouncy · Wellness · Aspirational-bakery · Optimized · Hand-holdy · Performative · Streaky · Lifestyle.

Precision is one of nine attributes — not the only one. It is qualified: *where it matters*. The bake itself doesn't need our precision; it needs our patience.

## 1.9 Brand pillars

Three. Every campaign, screen, feature, and post should advance at least one. If a feature serves none, kill it.

1. **The math is the floor.** Baker's percentages are how we speak. Every recipe in Baker Percentage is a precise, structured, comparable document. The math gets you to the door of your kitchen.
2. **The hand is the ceiling.** What happens in your kitchen — the folds, the smell at four hours of bulk, the way the dough fights you on shaping day — is yours. The app records it. The app does not perform it.
3. **The kitchen is the place.** Your conditions are the conditions. Your bread is your bread. We don't promise bakery-quality output; we promise that next month's bread, in your kitchen, will be better than last month's.

## 1.10 Values

Tie-breakers when decisions are close. Not posters.

- **Signal over volume.** A small library of beautiful formulas and bakes beats a large library of average ones.
- **The unit is the formula.** Not the user, not the post, not the like.
- **Defaults matter more than features.** What we ship turned on, set to, named, and ordered — that's the brand. Toggles are an admission of defeat.
- **No ads, ever.** Not a value. A contract.
- **Patience over engagement.** The app's job is not to bring you back tomorrow. It's to be here when you bake.
- **The bread, not the streak.** We don't measure success by daily active use. We measure it by whether your tenth loaf is better than your first.

# Part 2 — Verbal identity

## 2.1 Voice — two registers

### Formula register

When we speak about, on, or as a recipe. Precise. Quiet. Numbers where numbers help. No padding.

**Modifiers, in order of priority:**
1. **Precise.** Quantities are specific. Percentages are written out. No "a little" or "to taste."
2. **Spare.** No padding, no preamble, no story.
3. **Peer-level.** No instructional throat-clearing.

### Bake register

When we speak about, in, or as a diary entry — and most marketing copy. Warm. Conversational. Notes from one baker to another. Still no bullshit.

**Modifiers, in order of priority:**
1. **Honest.** Acknowledges the gap between recipe and loaf. "Felt tight at fold 3." "Kitchen was 28°C, bulk went fast."
2. **Particular.** Specific details (this flour, this hour, this loaf) over general claims.
3. **Generous.** To yourself, to the dough, to other bakers. Not effusive — generous.

### The rule

The voice can shift register *within a screen*. A recipe page is Formula. A diary entry on the same screen is Bake. The two don't fight — they sit next to each other the way a typed formula and a scribbled marginal note sit next to each other in a baker's notebook.

## 2.2 Tone per surface

Tone is voice + context. Voice doesn't change between surfaces; tone does.

| Surface                              | Register | Tone                              | Example                                                                |
| ------------------------------------ | -------- | --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Recipe / formula page                | Formula  | Declarative, factual              | "Hydration 75%. Salt 2.0%. Bulk 4h @ 26°C."                            |
| Marketing / landing hero             | Bake     | Confident, declarative, warm      | "The math is the floor. Your hands are the ceiling."                   |
| Marketing / explanation copy         | Bake     | Particular, generous              | "Your kitchen is the kitchen. We don't pretend it's a bakery."         |
| Onboarding (the only place we soften)| Bake     | Welcoming, still peer             | "Set your baseline flour weight. We'll do the percentages from here."  |
| Diary entry (user-written)           | Bake     | The user's voice — we just hold it| (whatever the user writes)                                              |
| Diary prompts (we suggest)           | Bake     | Generous, specific, optional      | "Anything you'd do differently?"                                       |
| Timer notifications                  | Formula  | Calm, factual, action-first       | "Bulk ends in 5 min. Shape next."                                      |
| Errors                               | Formula  | Direct, no apology theater        | "Didn't save. Tap to retry."                                           |
| Community guidelines                 | Bake     | Firm, generous, no jargon-hazing  | "Critique the recipe, not the baker."                                  |

## 2.3 Vocabulary

### Yes

`hydration` · `levain` · `bulk` · `shape` · `score` · `crumb` · `oven spring` · `pre-shape` · `final shape` · `retard` · `proof` · `autolyse` · `bake` · `formula` · `ratio` · `percentage` · `gram` · `notebook` · `diary` · `fold` · `tight` · `slack` · `windowpane` · `kitchen` · `ambient` · `humidity` · `the dough`

### No

`yummy` · `delicious` · `perfect` · `easy peasy` · `foolproof` · `step-by-step` · `tutorial` · `guide` · `journey` · `level up` · `unlock` · `streak` · `XP` · `bakery-quality` · `professional` (when used aspirationally) · `like a pro` · "Bread is *life*" or any variant · food emojis in body copy · the word "experience" used as a noun for the product

### Names for things in the product

This vocabulary is brand-defining. Use it everywhere. Never "log entry," never "post," never "session."

- A user-created recipe is a **formula**. (Pro bakeries call them this. We follow.)
- A version of a formula is a **revision**, numbered.
- A timer attached to a stage is a **stage** or a **stage timer**. (Not "alarm" in-product.)
- A shared formula visible to others is **published**.
- A group of formulas a baker is iterating on is a **batch**.
- A single baking session is a **bake**. (Each bake is attached to one formula.)
- The diary collection of all your bakes is your **notebook**. (Singular, possessive — "my notebook," "her notebook." A baker keeps one notebook.)
- An entry in your notebook is a **page**. (A bake = a page in the notebook.)
- Creating a new revision from someone else's formula is to **fork** it.

## 2.4 Taglines

Three candidates. Each does a different job.

1. **"Recipes in percentages. Bakes in your kitchen."** — *Recommended.* Carries both registers in one sentence. Six words, two halves, perfectly balanced.
2. **"The math is the floor. Your hands are the ceiling."** — More poetic, more philosophical. Better for hero shots than for the App Store subtitle.
3. **"Bread is not a cake recipe."** — Provocative, memorable, audience-filtering. Use as a campaign tagline or a manifesto opener, not as the everyday subtitle.

**Do not** use: "Bake better bread," "Your bread, perfected," "Bakery at home," "Bread, made simple," or anything ending in "made simple," "made easy," or "for everyone."

## 2.5 Manifesto

The founding text. Goes on the About page, in the first email, on a wall behind the founders if there ever is one. Long-form voice reference.

> **Bread is math. And then it's not.**
>
> Every bakery in the world writes its recipes the same way: flour is 100%, and every other ingredient is a percentage of that flour. It's the most useful piece of notation in any kitchen. Baker Percentage speaks this language. It is the floor of everything we do.
>
> But the math is not the bread. The math is what gets you to the door of your kitchen. What happens inside is the dough you have today — different flour than yesterday, different weather, a levain that was slow this morning. The recipe says four hours of bulk. The dough says three and a half. The recipe is your floor; your hands are your ceiling.
>
> Most baking apps don't believe this. They treat bread the way they treat cake — a deterministic equation where if you measure right, you get a result. Cakes work that way. Bread does not. We made the opposite kind of app.
>
> A precise recipe to start from. A diary to write into as you go: what the dough felt like, what the kitchen was, what you'd change. A timer that holds the long ferment so you can stop checking your phone. A small library of bakers who keep the same kind of notebook you do.
>
> The promise isn't that the bread you make today will be bakery-quality. Your kitchen is not a bakery, and we don't pretend it is. The promise is that the bread you make next month, in your specific kitchen, with your specific oven, will be better than the bread you made this month. Because you wrote down what happened, and because you read it before you baked again.
>
> Baker Percentage is for the home baker who's stopped following recipes and started writing them. You already know what hydration means. You also know the recipe isn't the bread.
>
> We made an app for that.

# Part 3 — Visual identity

The visual identity makes the two registers feel real. It refuses the wellness lookalike that dominates food brands and the OLED-with-neon lookalike that dominates tech apps. It puts Baker Percentage in the third place — editorial print, warm paper, deep ink, mono numbers — where almost nothing else lives.

What follows is the brand's argument for what visual choices have to do. The specifics — hex codes, named typefaces, sizing tokens, contrast ratios — live in the design system. The principles here are upstream of those choices and outlive them.

## 3.1 The symbol

The brand orbits a single mark — the **`%`** glyph. It is the most ownable visual asset in the system and appears everywhere the brand does: app icon, favicon, social profile, lockups, swag, future collaborations.

The symbol must survive from favicon scale to building-scale signage without redrawing. It is rendered in deep ink on warm paper by default; inversion (paper on ink) is allowed. No other color combinations exist. It is never decorated, never set inside a circle or rounded-rectangle background outside the app icon container, never combined with a wheat stalk, loaf illustration, or chef's hat.

## 3.2 Palette

**Two paper stocks, one ink, one hot accent.** That's the whole system.

The Formula register uses a lighter, cleaner paper — the canvas for recipes and the app default. The Bake register uses an older, slightly more humid paper — same warmth, more grain, used for diary entries and most marketing surfaces. Both are paper. Neither is white. The brand has no pure-white surface.

The ink is deep but not pure black. Warm enough to belong on the paper. The single accent is hot, surgical, never decorative — reserved for the one most important action on a screen, the live-recalculating number, the active timer's edge. It never covers more than a sliver of any surface. It never sits next to a second accent, because there is no second accent.

**Light is the default.** Most food apps default to cream-and-sage wellness; most tech apps default to OLED black with a neon accent. Both look generic. We sit in the editorial-print third place — warm paper, deep ink, hot accent. The brand reads like a cookbook page, not a wellness brand and not an AI startup.

Dark mode is a respected system setting, not the canvas. The dark canvas is warm cocoa-paper — never OLED — so the dark version still feels like the same brand printed on darker stock.

## 3.3 Type strategy

Three typographic roles. One family per role, used in strict accordance with what each one is for.

- **A display serif with character** carries recipe titles, hero copy, marquee moments, and — in its *italic* — the personal voice of the Bake register: diary titles, captions, the "Sunday morning." moments. The brand's voice when it speaks formally or warmly.
- **A precise neo-grotesque sans** carries UI labels, body copy, navigation, buttons. The brand's voice when it speaks plainly.
- **A monospaced face** is used exclusively for quantities — every percentage, gram, temperature, duration, ratio. This is the signature typographic move of the brand. If it's a number, it's in the mono. If it's prose, it's in the sans. There is no third option.

The same serif carries both the Formula register's formal moments and the Bake register's personal ones. The shift is in *use* — regular for the recipe, italic for the diary — not in *font*. One serif, one sans, one mono. The brand stays consistent; the Bake register's warmth comes from materiality, voice, and tempo — not from a different typeface.

The principle is what the doc commits to. Specific typefaces, weights, and sizing live in the design system and can be substituted for equivalents that serve the same role without changing the brand.

## 3.4 Materiality

**Materiality is the primary vehicle for the warmth the brand carries** — not photography, not voice alone, not lifestyle.

The two paper stocks are not just colors; they are *paper*. They have grain, weight, edges. Treat them like real paper. The Bake-register paper has slightly more grain than the Formula-register paper — it's older.

Photographs, when they appear, are *attached* to the page, not embedded in it — paper-bordered, slightly rotated, with a soft shadow. The page is the page; the photo is the photo. They sit together, but they don't fuse.

Margins matter. Diary pages breathe more than recipe pages — generous whitespace is a material choice, not an oversight. So is the rhythm at which they reveal (see §3.7).

Three rules:

- **Not skeuomorphism.** The app is not a fake notebook with leather corners. It is an app that *understands* a notebook.
- **Not lifestyle.** The texture serves the craft, not a vibe. If a texture starts feeling more "vibe" than "craft," cut it.
- **Not decoration.** Every material choice carries the brand's argument: the bake is hand-made, the diary is yours, the kitchen is the place. If a texture isn't doing that work, it's clutter.

## 3.5 Iconography

A monoline icon set. Geometric, not illustrative. The brand says "bread" through structure, not picture-language. No wheat stalks, no loaves, no rolling pins, no chef's hats. No AI-generated icons, no gradient icons, no icons with personality.

## 3.6 Photography

Used sparingly. The brand provides the frame; users provide the photos. A user's diary photo of their dough or their crumb is welcome — that's the user's hand, the user's bread, attached to the bake page they wrote. Stock photography, lifestyle photography of hands holding bowls or sourdough on marble with eucalyptus, and AI-generated bread imagery of any kind are forbidden.

Photography is *not* the vehicle of warmth in this brand. Materiality is. Photos appear only where the user puts them.

## 3.7 Motion — two tempos

Two motion vocabularies, matched to the two registers.

**The Formula register moves snappily.** Calm, precise, decisive. Numbers animate when they update. Layouts do not animate — they appear. Timers tick without overshoot. The active state of a stage is a single accent stroke, no celebration.

**The Bake register moves at kitchen time.** Slower. With breath. Things take the time bread takes. Entries fade and rise as if settling. Photographs drop in with a subtle final settle. Scroll is gentle, not snappy. There is always a pause between scroll-triggered reveals — never a cascade.

**Always:**

- Respect reduced motion. All animations collapse to instant.
- Numbers always animate when they change in either register.
- Layouts never animate.

**Never:**

- Spring physics with bounce.
- Page-level slide transitions.
- Snap scrolling.
- Parallax.
- Scroll-jacked storytelling.

Names are either **a baker's word** or **a precise English word**. Never marketing-speak.

# Appendix A — Risks to navigate

A real brand has real failure modes. Watch these.

### Risk 1: The name forecloses growth.

"Baker Percentage" filters out beginners. That's intentional. But if the business needs to broaden — to teaching, to other doughs, to a cooking app — the name will fight. **Mitigation:** don't broaden under this name. Sub-brands or sister products if growth demands it.

### Risk 2: The warm-paper register drifts into wellness.

Warm paper plus poor execution = cream-and-sage wellness brand. **Mitigation:** the Bake-register paper never appears without (a) mono numerals doing brand work somewhere on the same surface, (b) deep ink as the primary type color (never a softer brown), and (c) the hot accent used somewhere on the page if action is present. If any of these are missing on a Bake surface, the brand has slipped toward wellness.

### Risk 3: The two registers confuse the visual identity.

A brand with two visual languages risks looking like two brands. **Mitigation:** the Formula register and the Bake register share *everything* — same typography family, same color palette, same iconography, same hairlines, same accent rules. They differ only in canvas (the paper they sit on), motion tempo, and which voice of the serif leads (regular for Formula, italic for Bake). The two registers are the same brand on two different papers, set in two different voices of the same font.

### Risk 4: The peer-level voice reads cold.

The Formula register is calm and dry by design. To anyone outside the audience, calm can read as cold. **Mitigation:** the manifesto and Bake register copy do the warming work. If marketing reads cold, push more copy into the Bake register.

### Risk 5: The notebook becomes engagement bait.

The diary feature is the easiest place to slip into bad patterns — streaks, "you haven't baked in 7 days," anniversary nostalgia notifications, "share your bake!" prompts. **Mitigation:** Appendix B forbids all of these explicitly. The notebook is a notebook. It does not nag.

### Risk 6: Kitchen-time motion makes the app feel slow.

Slower motion can read as sluggish to anyone outside the target audience. **Mitigation:** the slower tempo is in the Bake register only. Formula content is still snappy. Timers are still precise.

# Appendix B — What this brand will NOT do

Decided in advance, so we don't relitigate.

- We will not run ads.
- We will not accept paid recipe placements.
- We will not add a beginner mode that ships under the Baker Percentage name.
- We will not gamify (no streaks, no badges, no XP, no levels, no anniversary notifications).
- We will not use AI-generated photography of bread.
- We will not use AI to write diary entries for users. The notebook is theirs.
- We will not add a generic "kitchen timer" feature unattached to a stage.
- We will not localize the recipe schema. Baker's percentages are universal.
- We will not collaborate with a celebrity baker. Bakers, yes. Celebrities, no.
- We will not promise "professional bread at home." We promise better bread, in your kitchen, over time.
- We will not nag. The app does not send "time to fold!" or "you haven't baked in a while" notifications. Stage timers ring; nothing else does.
- We will not algorithmically surface bakes. The notebook is chronological. The feed is chronological. No "For You."
- We will not make the diary social by default. Bakes are private. Publishing alongside a formula is one tap and one confirmation.
- We will not let onboarding teach baking. The app assumes you know how. Tutorials, if they exist someday, live elsewhere.
